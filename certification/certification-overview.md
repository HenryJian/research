# Food-safety certification overview for selling shiitake to supermarkets and distributors

Research date: 2026-04-28
Farm context: specialty mushroom farm in Waterford, Ontario; current crop is shiitake.

## Bottom line

Supermarkets and wholesale food distributors usually do not ask a shiitake vendor for a generic "food safety certificate." They usually ask for one of these, in roughly this order of seriousness:

1. **A GFSI-recognized food safety certification** for the farm/packing operation. This is the strongest and most supermarket-compatible answer.
2. **A buyer-approved annual third-party food safety audit** if the buyer does not strictly require GFSI recognition.
3. **Regulatory compliance documents** such as Safe Food for Canadians Regulations (SFCR) licensing/traceability/preventive-control evidence, product specifications, recall plan, insurance, and lot coding.
4. **Organic certification** only if the product will be sold as organic. Organic is not a substitute for food-safety certification.

For our shiitake farm, the most practical certification target is likely **SQF Food Safety Code: Primary Plant Production, FSC 2 - Indoor Growing and Harvesting of Fresh Produce and Sprouted Seed Crops**, because SQF explicitly includes mushroom farms and mushrooms in FSC 2. If the buyer or auditor treats our pack room as a separate packhouse activity, we may also need FSC 4 - Fresh Produce, Grain and Nut Pack House Operations.

**Do not assume CanadaGAP will solve this for our own shiitake production.** CanadaGAP is often requested by Canadian produce buyers, but its production/packing/storage crop list does not include mushrooms. CanadaGAP may be useful only for repacking, wholesaling, or brokerage of already market-ready mushrooms. See [CanadaGAP certification for a shiitake farm](canadagap.md) for the detailed scope analysis.

## What buyers are likely to ask for

| Buyer type | Likely food-safety ask | Practical meaning for us |
|---|---|---|
| National supermarkets and club stores | GFSI-recognized certification or the buyer's own approved audit standard | Prepare for SQF or another GFSI-recognized mushroom-compatible scheme before expecting a direct listing. |
| Supermarket private label / store brand | GFSI-recognized certification is especially likely | Sobeys reports annual GFSI-standard audits for Own Brands supplier sites. Similar expectations are common across major chains. |
| Walmart-style produce programs | Independently certified or assessed to a GFSI-recognized audit standard, unless Walmart grants an alternative | Walmart's fresh produce requirements explicitly cover operations from growing to final product. |
| Costco Canada | Costco-approved food safety certification/audit; SQF and CanadaGAP are named examples in BNQ's Costco Canada approval notice | For shiitake production, SQF is more relevant than CanadaGAP because CanadaGAP does not cover mushroom production. |
| Broadline foodservice distributors | Annual third-party quality, food-safety, and sanitation audit; sometimes GFSI depending on product, brand, or customer | Gordon Food Service says all suppliers require annual third-party quality, food-safety, and sanitation audits. |
| Produce wholesalers / terminal-market distributors | CanadaGAP, SQF, HACCP-based audit, vendor approval paperwork, and traceability | Some may accept a non-GFSI audit at first; larger accounts will push toward GFSI. |
| Restaurants / independent grocers | Usually no formal certification at first; they may ask for insurance, invoice traceability, and basic food-safety practices | Useful for early sales while building the system needed for bigger buyers. |

## Best-fit certifications for shiitake

### 1. SQF

**Recommended path for supermarket/distributor readiness.**

SQF is a GFSI-recognized certification program used across the food supply chain. Its Edition 10 food sector category list explicitly says FSC 2 applies to controlled-environment agriculture, including mushroom farms, and lists mushrooms as example products. FSC 2 covers production, harvesting, preparation, packaging, and on-site storage. FSC 4 covers packhouse activities such as packing, repacking, sorting, grading, loading, and on-site storage when the packhouse scope is separate or more complex.

Likely fit for us:

- **FSC 2**: indoor shiitake growing, fruiting, harvest, initial handling, packaging, and on-site cold storage.
- **FSC 4**: only if our packing/repacking/storage operation is scoped separately from the growing operation or if the certification body says the pack room needs this category.

What this signals to buyers:

- third-party audited food-safety management system,
- HACCP-based food safety plan,
- sanitation, pest control, worker hygiene, supplier approval, packaging control, traceability, recall readiness, complaints, corrective actions, and food defense/food fraud controls,
- certificate and audit report that large buyers can review.

### 2. PrimusGFS

**Possible alternative, especially if a buyer already uses Azzule/PrimusGFS.**

PrimusGFS is GFSI-recognized and widely used for produce. It covers the supply chain from pre-farm-gate to post-farm-gate production and includes Food Safety Management Systems, GAP, GMP, and HACCP elements. PrimusGFS certification bodies list scopes such as Farm, Indoor Agriculture, Harvest Crew, Storage and Distribution, Cooler/Cold Storage, Packinghouse, and Processing.

Likely fit for us:

- indoor agriculture / farm audit for shiitake growing,
- harvest crew controls if harvesting is separately scoped,
- packinghouse or cooler/cold storage if required for the pack/cooler area.

Buyer note: choose PrimusGFS if a target buyer specifically accepts or prefers it, or if a certification body can confirm a clean mushroom scope for our exact operation.

Detailed farm-specific analysis: [PrimusGFS assessment for a shiitake farm](primusgfs-shiitake.md).

### 3. GLOBALG.A.P.

**Possible but should be confirmed before spending money.**

GLOBALG.A.P. IFA is widely recognized by retailers for fruit and vegetable production, but mushroom scope can be trickier than SQF because mushrooms are fungi rather than plants. If a buyer suggests GLOBALG.A.P., get written scope confirmation from a certification body for cultivated shiitake, including growing, harvesting, packing, and cold storage.

### 4. CanadaGAP

**Common Canadian produce buyer language, but not a good fit for our own shiitake production.**

CanadaGAP is a HACCP-based Canadian produce food-safety certification program, and options B, C, and D are GFSI-recognized. Canadian produce buyers including Metro, Loblaw, and Walmart have been listed by CanadaGAP as endorsing CanadaGAP along with other GFSI-recognized programs.

However, CanadaGAP's production/packing/storage commodity list does not include mushrooms, and the CanadaGAP Program Management Manual identifies mushrooms as outside production/packing/storage scope. For our own shiitake crop, this means CanadaGAP is not the certificate to pursue unless CanadaGAP or a licensed certification body gives written confirmation that the scope has changed.

Possible limited uses:

- **CanadaGAP Option D**: repacking or wholesaling fresh fruits and vegetables; GFSI-recognized. CanadaGAP says Option D covers repacking/wholesaling except fresh sprouts, repacking of enoki mushrooms, hermetically sealed containers, and minimally processed products.
- **CanadaGAP Option F**: repacking, wholesaling, or brokerage when the customer does not require GFSI recognition.

These would cover repacking/wholesale/brokerage controls, not our mushroom production.

### 5. HACCP or GMP/GAP audit without GFSI recognition

**Useful stepping stone, not the strongest supermarket answer.**

Some regional distributors may accept an annual third-party HACCP/GMP/GAP audit, especially before the farm is large enough to justify full GFSI certification. This may help with vendor approval, but it can be rejected by national supermarkets or private-label programs that require a GFSI-recognized certificate.

## Regulatory documents buyers may ask for

These are not the same as buyer certification, but they will likely be part of vendor approval.

### Safe Food for Canadians Regulations

CFIA treats fresh specialty mushrooms, including shiitake and oyster mushrooms, under the **fresh fruits and vegetables** commodity category for licensing guidance. Under the SFCR:

- Growing or harvesting fresh fruits or vegetables for **interprovincial trade** does not itself require an SFC licence, but preventive controls and traceability apply, and a written preventive control plan (PCP) is required if gross annual food sales are over CAD $100,000.
- Packaging and labelling fresh fruits or vegetables for **interprovincial trade** generally requires an SFC licence unless the product will be subsequently manufactured, processed, treated, preserved, or graded by a licence holder in another province.
- Importing fresh fruits or vegetables requires an SFC licence.
- Selling fresh fruits or vegetables only to consumers at retail within Ontario does not require an SFC licence under the CFIA table, but traceability still applies.

For our sales planning, assume that selling to a supermarket or distributor outside Ontario, or packing/labelling consumer packages for interprovincial trade, may trigger licensing and written PCP obligations. Confirm the exact requirement with CFIA before shipping outside Ontario.

### Fresh mushroom packaging

Health Canada warns that fresh mushrooms in trays completely wrapped with non-perforated plastic film can create botulism risk. Health Canada recommends visibly perforated plastic film or equivalent air exchange, and says hermetically sealed fresh mushrooms must be refrigerated at 4 C or less, labelled "Keep Refrigerated" / "Garder au froid," and marked with a clear best-before date.

This matters because buyers may review our packaging specification, case label, temperature requirement, and shelf-life basis.

## Minimum vendor approval packet to prepare

Before approaching supermarkets or larger distributors, prepare a clean document packet:

- current food-safety certificate, ideally SQF or another buyer-accepted GFSI-recognized certificate,
- latest full audit report and corrective action closure, if the buyer requests it,
- SFC licence, if required for our activity,
- written preventive control plan or HACCP-style food safety plan,
- recall procedure and mock recall record,
- lot coding and traceability procedure showing one step back and one step forward,
- product specification for each SKU: shiitake size/grade, pack size, packaging material, storage temperature, shelf life, allergen statement, and intended use,
- packaging proof showing perforated film or other approved mushroom ventilation approach,
- sanitation SOPs and records for harvest tools, packing surfaces, totes, cooler, and delivery vehicle,
- pest-control program and inspection records,
- employee hygiene and illness policy,
- water test results if water contacts mushrooms, food-contact surfaces, humidification systems, or cleaning processes,
- cooler temperature logs and delivery temperature records,
- supplier approval records for substrate blocks, packaging, cleaning chemicals, and any outsourced storage/transport,
- complaint/deviation/corrective-action log,
- certificate of insurance naming the buyer if required,
- organic certificate only for organic claims.

## Recommended path for the farm

1. **Ask target buyers what they accept before buying an audit.** Use exact language: "For an Ontario indoor shiitake farm that grows, harvests, packs, cools, and delivers fresh shiitake, do you require GFSI-recognized certification? If yes, which schemes and scopes do you accept?"
2. **Treat SQF as the default target.** Ask SQF certification bodies to quote FSC 2 for indoor mushroom production and to confirm whether our pack/cooler area also needs FSC 4.
3. **Use PrimusGFS as the main alternate.** It may be a good fit where buyers already manage supplier audits through Azzule/PrimusGFS.
4. **Do not pursue CanadaGAP for our own shiitake production unless the scope changes in writing.** If a buyer says "CanadaGAP," explain that CanadaGAP does not cover mushroom production/first packing and ask whether SQF FSC 2 or PrimusGFS would satisfy the same food-safety requirement.
5. **Build the food-safety system before the audit.** The work is not just passing inspection; it is records, procedures, traceability, corrective actions, training, and management review operating consistently.
6. **Use restaurants, independent grocers, and local distributors as stepping stones.** They can help validate packaging, shelf life, delivery temperature controls, and recall/traceability records before the cost of a full GFSI audit.

## Buyer wording cheat sheet

If a buyer asks, "Are you food-safety certified?":

> We are preparing for SQF certification under the Primary Plant Production code for indoor mushroom production. SQF FSC 2 explicitly covers mushroom farms and mushrooms. If your supplier program requires a specific GFSI-recognized scheme or audit scope, please send the accepted schemes and any produce/mushroom-specific requirements so we can align the audit scope correctly.

If a buyer asks specifically for CanadaGAP:

> CanadaGAP is commonly used for Canadian produce, but it does not currently cover mushroom production, packing, and storage for our own shiitake crop. Would your food-safety team accept SQF FSC 2 for indoor mushroom production or another GFSI-recognized mushroom-compatible scheme instead?
