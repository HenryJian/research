# PrimusGFS assessment for a shiitake farm

Research date: 2026-04-28

Farm context: specialty mushroom farm in Waterford, Ontario; current crop is shiitake, with oyster and maitake planned.

## Bottom line

PrimusGFS is a credible and likely compatible GFSI-recognized route for an indoor shiitake farm. Unlike CanadaGAP production certification, PrimusGFS explicitly lists mushroom farms under its Indoor Agriculture scope. For fresh whole shiitake grown indoors, harvested by farm staff, packed on site, cooled, and delivered, the likely audit structure is:

- **Module 1, Food Safety Management System**, plus
- **Module 3, Indoor Agriculture**, for growing and harvesting, and probably
- **Module 5, GMP**, plus **Module 6, HACCP**, if the farm's pack room, cooler, or shipping area is included as Packinghouse, Cooler/Cold Storage, or Storage and Distribution.

The eligibility question looks favorable. The difficulty question is different: gaining PrimusGFS is medium-hard to hard for a small shiitake farm unless the farm already has disciplined records, hygienic zoning, a clean pack room, supplier controls, water controls, traceability, sanitation, pest control, recall readiness, and corrective-action habits.

Practical recommendation: treat PrimusGFS as the best alternate to SQF, especially if a target buyer accepts or prefers Azzule/PrimusGFS. Do not buy an audit until a certification body confirms the exact mushroom scope in writing and the farm has at least three months of records that match the relevant modules.

## Version and recognition caution

PrimusGFS v3.2 is the version most clearly tied to current GFSI recognition in public sources reviewed on 2026-04-28. PrimusGFS has released v4.0 documents and says v4.0 was going through the GFSI benchmarking process during 2025. Because certification standards transition over time, confirm three points before contracting:

1. Which PrimusGFS version will be audited.
2. Whether that version and scope are GFSI-recognized for the buyer's program.
3. Whether the certificate can explicitly identify the operation as indoor shiitake mushroom growing, harvesting, packing, and cold storage.

This matters because GFSI category language is often plant-oriented, while PrimusGFS program documents explicitly include mushroom farms. The safest path is a written scope confirmation from the certification body and, ideally, written buyer acceptance.

## Likely scope for our shiitake operation

## Compatibility analysis

### Why PrimusGFS fits shiitake better than CanadaGAP

CanadaGAP production/packing/storage scope does not currently include mushrooms. PrimusGFS, by contrast, has a specific Indoor Agriculture path that includes mushroom farms. That makes PrimusGFS a real candidate for certifying our own shiitake production, not just repacking or wholesaling.

### Why it fits indoor shiitake operationally

Indoor shiitake production is controlled-environment produce handling in the practical sense that buyers care about:

- product is fresh and perishable,
- workers harvest by hand,
- product may go directly into consumer or foodservice packs,
- coolers and delivery practices affect shelf life and safety,
- packaging choices matter because fresh mushrooms can create botulism risk if sealed without oxygen exchange,
- traceability must connect substrate or block lots, harvest dates, pack lots, and customer shipments.

PrimusGFS is built around these kinds of produce-chain controls: food safety management, GAP, GMP, HACCP, sanitation, worker hygiene, water, chemicals, pest control, storage, traceability, recall, corrective actions, and supplier approval.

### Main compatibility caveats

PrimusGFS should be treated as compatible, but not automatic:

- **Buyer acceptance controls the value of the certificate.** Some buyers may name SQF, CanadaGAP, or "any GFSI-recognized scheme." If a buyer says "CanadaGAP" for shiitake, ask whether PrimusGFS Indoor Agriculture or SQF FSC 2 is accepted instead.
- **The certificate scope must include the right activities.** A farm-only certificate may not satisfy a buyer buying packed, labelled product. If we pack and cool on site, include the pack/cooler scope if possible.
- **Version transition risk is real.** Confirm v3.2 versus v4.0, GFSI recognition, and buyer acceptance before signing a quote.
- **Substrate production can widen the audit.** Buying ready-to-fruit blocks is easier to document than making substrate in-house. If we make blocks, supplier approval, ingredient control, sterilization/pasteurization records, spawn handling, inoculation hygiene, incubation, contamination disposal, and lot linkage become more important.
- **Processing changes the risk class.** Fresh whole shiitake is the cleanest fit. Drying, slicing, vacuum packaging, oil packing, or ready-to-eat prepared products should be scoped separately.

## What the farm would need to prove

### Food safety management system

The farm needs a documented system that actually operates. Likely evidence includes:

- food-safety policy and assigned food-safety responsibility,
- current procedures and controlled forms,
- internal audit and management review,
- product descriptions and intended use,
- supplier approval for substrate blocks, spawn, sawdust, bran/supplements, packaging, chemicals, pest-control service, labs, and outsourced transport/storage,
- traceability and recall procedure, including mock recall,
- complaints, deviations, corrective actions, and product hold/release,
- food defense, food fraud, crisis management, and training records,
- document retention and version control.

For a small farm, this is often the hardest cultural shift. The auditor is not only checking whether the rooms look clean on audit day; they are checking whether the farm can prove it controlled risk over time.

### Site and growing rooms

For indoor shiitake, expect scrutiny around:

- site maps, room identification, product flow, employee flow, waste flow, and traffic flow,
- separation between dirty/substrate areas and harvest/packing/finished-product areas,
- pest exclusion and monitoring,
- cull or contaminated-block disposal,
- condensation, dripping, standing water, drain condition, and cleaning access,
- lighting protection and brittle-plastic/glass control,
- wall, floor, ceiling, rack, cart, bin, tote, and tool cleanability,
- employee and visitor hygiene controls,
- records that connect growing room, block lot, harvest date, and finished lot.

Outdoor log-grown shiitake would likely be more complicated than indoor block production because of wildlife, soil, weather, water, and field sanitation exposure. For the farm's planned indoor specialty mushroom context, PrimusGFS is a better fit.

### Substrate, blocks, spawn, and growing media

Shiitake is unusual compared with many produce crops because the "growing medium" may be a sterile or pasteurized supplemented hardwood block, or a log system. Important controls:

- approved suppliers for blocks, spawn, sawdust, bran, gypsum, bags, filters, and supplements,
- receiving checks for block condition, contamination, temperature if relevant, lot code, and supplier documentation,
- storage controls that prevent contamination before fruiting,
- if blocks are made in-house: formula records, water source, sterilization/pasteurization cycle records, inoculation hygiene, spawn lot records, incubation records, and contaminated-block disposal,
- lot linking from input materials or block lots to harvest and shipment lots.

Buying ready-to-fruit blocks can make certification much easier because it shifts part of the biological-process control to an approved supplier. It does not remove the need for supplier approval and traceability.

### Water, humidity, and condensation

Water can contact mushrooms directly, contact food-contact surfaces, or create aerosols through humidifiers. The farm should be ready with:

- water source identification and backflow protection,
- water risk assessment,
- microbiological water testing based on use and risk,
- cleaning and maintenance records for humidifiers, nozzles, tanks, hoses, and drains,
- controls for condensate above product, packaging, racks, or food-contact surfaces,
- potable water evidence where water contacts harvested product or food-contact surfaces.

Even if mushrooms are not washed, humidification and sanitation water still matter.

### Harvesting and worker hygiene

Likely expectations:

- employee hygiene and illness policy,
- training records for harvest, packing, handwashing, glove use if used, and reporting illness or wounds,
- visitor and contractor rules,
- handwashing and toilet access,
- clean harvest knives/scissors, totes, carts, and gloves,
- controls for dropped product, bodily-fluid events, glass breakage, pests, chemicals, and foreign material,
- lot-coded harvest records by room, date, and block or production batch.

Small shiitake farms often underestimate the recordkeeping around normal daily harvest work. PrimusGFS will make that routine visible.

### Packing, packaging, and cooler

If packed shiitake is sold to retailers or distributors, the pack/cooler area should be certification-ready:

- pack room designed for cleanable surfaces and controlled traffic,
- packaging stored clean, covered, off the floor, and traceable by lot where possible,
- finished product protected from substrate, soil, pests, chemicals, waste, and condensation,
- cleaning and sanitation SOPs for tables, scales, tools, harvest totes, carts, cooler, drains, and vehicles,
- chemical list, SDS access, sanitizer concentration checks, and labelled chemical storage,
- thermometer and scale calibration or verification,
- cooler temperature logs, corrective actions for excursions, and loading checks,
- product hold/release process,
- finished lot code on cases and/or consumer packs,
- shipping records that support one-step-forward traceability.

Mushroom packaging needs special attention. Health Canada warns that completely wrapped, non-perforated fresh mushroom packages can create conditions for botulism toxin formation. Use visibly perforated film or an equivalent air-exchange design unless a validated packaging approach says otherwise. Hermetically sealed fresh mushrooms require refrigeration at 4 C or less, "Keep Refrigerated/Garder au froid" labelling, and a clear best-before date.

## How easy or hard is it?

### Overall difficulty

For our likely fresh whole shiitake use case, PrimusGFS is **eligible but not easy**.

| Starting condition | Likely difficulty | Practical preparation time |
|---|---:|---|
| Indoor farm buys ready-to-fruit blocks, has a clean pack room/cooler, already keeps harvest/lot/cooler/sanitation records | Medium | 3 to 6 months |
| Indoor farm makes its own blocks but has good physical separation and disciplined records | Medium-hard | 6 to 9 months |
| Farm has good product but informal records, mixed-use rooms, limited sanitation logs, no mock recall, and no written supplier program | Hard | 6 to 12 months |
| Farm wants fresh plus sliced/dried/vacuum-packed/oil-packed/value-added mushrooms | Harder | Scope separately; do not assume the fresh mushroom audit covers this |

These are practical planning estimates, not PrimusGFS rules. Audit readiness depends on the gap between the current operation and the standard.

### What makes it easier for shiitake

- Indoor production means no open-field wildlife, manure runoff, field toilets, or rain-splash exposure.
- Shiitake grown on hardwood blocks can be simpler than compost-based mushroom systems, especially if blocks are purchased from controlled suppliers.
- The farm can run year-round, which may make audit timing easier than seasonal field crops.
- A narrow SKU set and direct ownership of growing, packing, cooling, and delivery can simplify traceability.
- PrimusGFS has a clear produce-chain structure and a direct mushroom-farm scope.

### What makes it hard

- The farm must prove routine control through records, not just explain good practices.
- The audit score threshold is high: PrimusGFS uses scored modules, and public v3.2 regulations describe passing thresholds at the total and module level.
- Certain issues can cause automatic failure or certification denial, especially severe food-safety risks or inability to correct non-conformances.
- Corrective actions must be handled quickly after the audit; the public v3.2 regulations describe a 30-calendar-day corrective-action window.
- Pack room and cooler design may need upgrades before an audit makes sense.
- Water, humidification, condensation, and reusable containers can become real control points.
- Linking substrate/block lots to harvest lots and customer shipments may require changing harvest and packing paperwork.
- Certification is annual, so the system has to be maintained, not staged once.

## Roadmap to gain PrimusGFS

### 1. Confirm buyer and certification-body acceptance

Ask the buyer:

> For an Ontario indoor shiitake mushroom farm that grows, harvests, first-packs, cools, and delivers fresh whole shiitake, do you accept PrimusGFS as a GFSI-recognized certification? If yes, which operation scopes and modules must appear on the certificate?

Ask the certification body:

> Can you certify cultivated indoor shiitake mushrooms under PrimusGFS Indoor Agriculture, including growing, harvesting, first packing, cooler storage, and shipping from one site in Ontario? Which PrimusGFS version, GFSI-recognized scopes, operation types, and modules would appear on the certificate?

Keep both written answers in this folder.

### 2. Define the exact scope before building documents

The most likely target scope for a fresh shiitake certificate is:

- Product: fresh whole shiitake mushrooms.
- Site: Waterford, Ontario farm.
- Activities: indoor growing/fruiting, harvesting, packing, cooling, storage, and shipping.
- Modules: Module 1 + Module 3, plus Module 5 + Module 6 if pack/cooler is in scope.
- Exclusions: wild mushrooms, value-added products, drying, slicing, cooking, oil packing, vacuum packing, and brokered third-party mushrooms unless explicitly included.

Do not leave pack/cooler scope ambiguous. The certificate should match how the product is sold.

### 3. Run a gap assessment against the checklists

Use the current PrimusGFS question-and-expectation documents for the version the certification body will audit. Build a gap list in these categories:

- documents and records,
- building and equipment,
- water and humidity,
- substrate and supplier approval,
- sanitation and chemicals,
- worker hygiene and training,
- pest control,
- harvest and packing practices,
- cold chain and shipping,
- traceability and recall,
- HACCP or food-safety plan,
- corrective actions and internal audit.

Rank gaps by whether they require construction, purchasing, procedure writing, staff training, or only form cleanup.

### 4. Build the operating system

At minimum, create and run:

- food-safety manual or procedure index,
- site map, room map, product flow, employee flow, and waste flow,
- supplier approval program,
- substrate/block receiving log,
- water test plan and results,
- cleaning and sanitation SOPs and logs,
- pest-control program and inspection log,
- employee training and hygiene records,
- visitor and contractor log,
- harvest and pack records with lot codes,
- packaging receiving and use records,
- cooler temperature logs,
- delivery sanitation and temperature checks,
- chemical inventory, SDS binder, sanitizer checks, and calibration records,
- hold/release, deviation, corrective-action, complaint, and mock recall records,
- annual internal audit and management review.

This system should be used for real production before the audit. PrimusGFS public guidance for initial applicants points to having at least three months of records.

### 5. Perform a mock trace and mock recall before scheduling

A strong mock recall should trace:

- from a finished case or clamshell back to harvest date, room, block/substrate lot, packaging lot, and relevant worker/shift records,
- from a block or packaging lot forward to all finished lots and customers,
- within a target time acceptable to the buyer and certification body.

If the mock recall cannot be completed cleanly, the farm is not audit-ready yet.

### 6. Consider a pre-assessment

For a first GFSI-style certification, a non-certification pre-assessment can save money and frustration. Ask whether the certification body can provide a pre-assessment without compromising auditor independence, or use a consultant familiar with PrimusGFS and mushroom/CEA operations.

### 7. Schedule the certification audit while activities are operating

The audit should occur when growing, harvesting, packing, cooling, and shipping are visible. An auditor cannot meaningfully verify activities that are not happening or supported by recent records.

### 8. Close corrective actions and maintain the system

After the audit, respond to non-conformances with root cause, correction, corrective action, responsible person, completion date, and evidence. Then keep the system running for the next annual audit.

## Scope questions to send to certification bodies

Use this exact checklist when requesting quotes:

1. Do you certify indoor shiitake mushroom farms under PrimusGFS?
2. Which version of PrimusGFS would apply today, and is that version GFSI-recognized for the proposed scope?
3. Which operation type would you use: Indoor Agriculture, Harvest Crew, Packinghouse, Cooler/Cold Storage, Storage and Distribution, or another scope?
4. Can growing, harvesting, first packing, cooling, and shipping be covered in one audit and one certificate?
5. Would Module 5 and Module 6 be required for our pack room and cooler?
6. Would the audit cover purchased ready-to-fruit blocks differently from in-house block production?
7. What records must exist before an initial audit, and how many months of records do you expect?
8. What water, product, and environmental testing do you expect for indoor shiitake?
9. Do you have auditors with mushroom or controlled-environment agriculture experience in Ontario?
10. What are the audit, certification, platform, travel, follow-up, and annual renewal costs?
11. What is the expected audit duration for our scope?
12. What exact wording would appear on the certificate for product and activities?

## Buyer wording

If a buyer asks whether we are certified:

> We are evaluating PrimusGFS for our indoor shiitake operation. PrimusGFS explicitly includes mushroom farms under Indoor Agriculture, and we are confirming scope for growing, harvesting, packing, cooler storage, and shipping of fresh whole shiitake. Please confirm whether your supplier program accepts PrimusGFS for this mushroom scope, or whether you require SQF or another GFSI-recognized scheme.

If a buyer asks specifically for CanadaGAP:

> CanadaGAP does not currently cover mushroom production, packing, or storage for our own shiitake crop. Would your food-safety team accept PrimusGFS Indoor Agriculture or SQF FSC 2 for indoor mushroom production instead?

## Decision for the farm

PrimusGFS is compatible enough to pursue seriously, but only after buyer and certification-body confirmation. For our farm, the main decision is not "Can shiitake fit PrimusGFS?" It probably can. The decision is whether the target buyer will accept PrimusGFS and whether we are ready to operate at GFSI-documentation discipline.

Recommended next actions:

1. Ask target buyers whether PrimusGFS is accepted for indoor cultivated mushrooms.
2. Ask at least two PrimusGFS certification bodies to confirm scope and quote Module 1 + Module 3, with pack/cooler included if needed.
3. In parallel, build a certification-ready food-safety system because the same work will also support SQF if the buyer prefers SQF.
4. Keep fresh whole shiitake separate from any value-added mushroom products until certification scope is clear.
5. Do not schedule the certification audit until records, mock recall, water controls, sanitation, pest control, supplier approval, cooler logs, and pack-room controls have run cleanly for at least three months.
